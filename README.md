COMPANY OVERVIEW

Amazon.com, Inc. is one of the world’s largest e-commerce, cloud computing, and logistics companies. The company operates across multiple business areas, including online retail, Amazon Web Services, digital advertising, subscription services, fulfillment, and transportation. At peak periods, Amazon fulfills approximately 8 million packages per day. A major part of Amazon’s competitive advantage is its ability to deliver products quickly and reliably to customers. In its 2024 annual report, Amazon reported total revenue growth from $575 billion to $638 billion, showing the scale and continued expansion of its business operations. Amazon’s logistics network operates more than 1,100 fulfillment, sortation, and delivery station facilities globally; a fleet of over 100 Amazon Air cargo aircraft; more than 30,000 branded delivery vans; and a network of over 200,000 Amazon Flex independent contractors and Delivery Service Partner (DSP) associates.  

Despite this scale, last-mile delivery remains Amazon's single largest operational cost center, estimated at $14 to $17 per residential package, and its most complex risk domain. The fundamental challenge is optimization: with millions of daily stops distributed across diverse urban, suburban, and rural geographies. Amazon's current route optimization infrastructure, while sophisticated, was built incrementally over two decades and lacks the unified AI-driven optimization intelligence required to remain competitive against a new generation of last-mile technology disruptors. For this reason, Amazon is considering acquiring an AI-powered route optimization service that can improve routing decisions, reduce delivery delays, lower costs, and make delivery operations more efficient. 

 
ERM RISK UNDER CONSIDERATION
The primary ERM risk is strategic and operational integration risk: Amazon could acquire an AI last-mile optimization service but failed to integrate it safely, securely, and effectively into its fulfillment, driver, Delivery Service Partner (DSP), customer communication, and data governance systems. Secondary risks span people (driver and DSP resistance, talent gaps), processes (data quality, change management, regulatory compliance), technology (model drift, API reliability, algorithmic bias), facilities (delivery station readiness, vehicle compatibility), and information security (data breach, unauthorized access, cyber disruption of routing infrastructure). 

If unmanaged, these risks could collectively lead to poor delivery decisions, cybersecurity incidents, regulatory investigations, driver resistance, reputational damage, and reduced return on investment outcomes that would undermine both the strategic rationale and financial value of the acquisition. 
 

BRAINSTORMING TECHNIQUES USED TO IDENTIFY RISKS  
The following brainstorming techniques were used to identify the risk themes behind the SWOT analysis: 
Stakeholder mapping: Identified affected groups, including customers, drivers, Delivery Service Partners (DSPs), operations managers, data scientists, cybersecurity teams, regulators, and competitors. 
PESTLE scanning: Reviewed political/regulatory, economic, social, technological, legal, and environmental pressures affecting AI-enabled last-mile delivery. 
Pre-mortem analysis: Assumed the acquisition failed two years after closing and worked backward to identify likely causes such as weak data quality, poor change management, cyber exposure, and regulatory objections. 
Process mapping: Considered how an order moves from the fulfillment center to customer delivery and where AI routing could create or reduce risk. 
KRI brainstorming: Converted each risk theme into measurable warning indicators that management could monitor before, during, and after acquisition integration.
